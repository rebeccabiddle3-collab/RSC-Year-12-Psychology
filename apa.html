APA Referencing Guide

Everything you need to reference correctly — formats, examples, word count rules, and common mistakes.

Word Count — What's In & What's Out

The 2000 word limit is absolute. No ±10%.
▼

✅ INCLUDED in word count

  • All words in the text of the response
  • Title, headings and subheadings
  • Tables/figures/diagrams containing information other than raw or processed data
  • Quotations
  • Footnotes and endnotes (unless bibliographical)

❌ EXCLUDED from word count

  • Title pages
  • Contents pages
  • Abstract
  • Raw or processed data in tables, figures, diagrams
  • Bibliography / reference list
  • Appendixes*
  • Page numbers
  • In-text citations
⚠️ Important: You must include a word count at the end of your report. Appendixes should only contain supplementary material not used directly as evidence when marking. The word count is absolute — no +/- 10% allowed.
💬

In-Text Citations — Two Types

Use Author, Year only — not full title or credentials
▼
Key rule: APA formatting only requires the author's surname and the year of publication. Don't include titles, credentials, or institutional affiliations in your citations.

Narrative citation

"Grant et al. (1998) found that…"

Author name is part of your sentence. Year in parentheses after name.

Parenthetical citation

"…performance was better in matching conditions (Grant et al., 1998)."

Both author and year in parentheses at the end. Use & inside brackets.

What must be cited:

  • Ideas, opinions and theories of authors — paraphrased or directly quoted
  • Direct quotes of spoken or written words
  • Factual information not considered common knowledge (statistics, data)
  • Visual information including graphs, drawings and photographs
Conciseness tip: Saved 12 words from one sentence by removing credentials! Don't write "an experiment conducted by Grant and colleagues at Iowa State University in (1998)" — just write "Grant et al. (1998)".

Reference Format — Journal Article

Most common source type for Psychology IA2
▼
Author, A. A., & Author, B. B. (Year). Title of the article. Name of the Periodical, volume(issue), page–page. https://doi.org/xxxx

Example:

Pope, J., & Owen, A. D. (2009). Emission trading schemes: Potential revenue effects, compliance costs and overall tax policy issues. Energy Policy, 37(4), 4595–4603. https://doi.org/10.1016/j.enpol.2009.06.014

  • Last name first, followed by comma and initials. Space between initials.
  • Year in parentheses, followed by full stop
  • Article title — capitalise only first word and proper nouns. No italics.
  • Journal name — capitalise all major words. Italicise the journal name and volume number
  • Issue number in parentheses — do NOT italicise. No space before parentheses.
  • Include DOI for all works that have one. No full stop after DOI/URL.
📚

Reference Format — Book

Including textbook citations
▼
Author, A. A., & Author, B. B. (Copyright Year). Title of the book (7th ed.). Publisher. DOI or URL

Example:

Mack, C. (2005). Looking at the Renaissance: Essays toward a conceptual appreciation. Taylor & Francis.

  • Italicise the book title. Capitalise only first word and proper nouns.
  • Include edition number in parentheses after title (if applicable)
  • Include publisher name — do NOT include publisher location
  • Include DOI if available; include URL for ebooks from other websites
  • If multiple publishers, separate with semicolons
📑

Reference Format — Chapter in Edited Book

When citing a specific chapter, not the whole book
▼
Author, A. A. (Year). Title of chapter. In A. A. Editor & B. B. Editor (Eds.), Title of the book (2nd ed., pp. #–#). Publisher. DOI or URL
  • Write "In" then the editor initials and surname (not inverted)
  • Use "(Ed.)" for one editor, "(Eds.)" for multiple editors, then comma
  • Include chapter page range — end with period
  • Include edition and volume number if applicable

Reference Format — Webpage, Wikipedia & Dictionary

Other common sources
▼
Source Format Example
Webpage Author, A. A. (Year). Title of page. Retrieved from URL Steel, W. (2008). Hints for web authors. Retrieved from http://www.mcsr.olemiss.edu/~mudws/webhints.html

Psychology-Specific Sources (DSM, scales, instruments)

Use acronym after first mention
▼

For named scales and manuals, introduce the full name and acronym on first mention, then use the acronym only:

First mention:

The Diagnostic and Statistical Manual of Mental Disorders, Fifth Edition (DSM-5; American Psychiatric Association, 2013)

Subsequent mentions:

DSM-5 (American Psychiatric Association, 2013)

Same rule applies to: DASS-21 (Lovibond & Lovibond, 1995), PSQI (Buysse et al., 1989), and any other named instrument.

📋

Reference List Format & Layout

Start on a new page · Alphabetical order · Double-spaced
▼
  • Start on a new page. Heading: References — bold, centred (first-level heading)
  • Double-spaced throughout, with a hanging indent (first line flush left, subsequent lines indented)
  • Arranged in alphabetical order by first author's surname
  • Every in-text citation must appear in the reference list — and vice versa
  • Report formatting: 12pt, Times New Roman, double spacing
Common mistake: In-text citations that don't match the reference list (e.g. citing "Encyclopedia Britannica, 2017" but listing an author name in the reference). Make sure the author name matches exactly in both places.

Scientific Language & Genre Rules

What "fluent and concise" actually looks like
▼

✅ Good scientific writing

  • Third person, past tense throughout
  • Correct psychological terminology used naturally
  • Statistics italicised (e.g. p = .05, M = 4.2)
  • Acronyms defined on first use (e.g. CBT)
  • Concise — no unnecessary words
  • Table headings above; figure captions below
  • All figures and tables referenced in text

❌ Common errors

  • Writing "we" / "I" / "you"
  • Using present tense for what happened in your experiment
  • Copying text directly from a source (plagiarism)
  • In-text citations not matching reference list
  • Missing citations for ideas or facts
  • Spelling errors in author names
  • Stand-alone tables/figures with no description in text
Plagiarism warning: You need to write the report in your OWN words. Copying sentences from a scaffold, another student, or a source — even with slight changes — is plagiarism and will be detected by Turnitin.
