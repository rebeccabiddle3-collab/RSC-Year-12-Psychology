Theory & Concepts

The psychological knowledge that underpins your experiment — variables, limitations, uncertainty, reliability and validity.

The Original Study

Grant, H. M., Bredahl, L. C., Clay, J., Ferrie, J., Groves, J. E., McDorman, T. A., & Dark, V. J. (1998)

Context-dependent memory for meaningful material: Information for students. Applied Cognitive Psychology, 12, 617–623.

Participants: 39 participants (17F, 23M), age 17–56, M = 23.4
Design: Between-subjects factorial (2×2)
IV 1: Study context (silent vs noisy)
IV 2: Test context (silent vs noisy)
DV: Score on short-answer (out of 10) and multiple-choice test (out of 16)
Material: Two-page article on psychoimmunology

Key Results

  • ✅ Matching conditions outperformed mismatching on both test types
  • ✅ Short-answer: silent/silent M=6.7, noisy/noisy M=6.2 vs mismatching M≈5.0
  • ✅ Multiple-choice: matching M=14.3 vs mismatching M=12.7
  • ✅ Significant Study × Test interaction for both tests (p < .05)
  • ⚠️ No overall main effect of noise on performance

Conclusion: students may perform better on exams by studying in silence.

Why theory matters in your report

Your report isn't just about your data — it must connect your findings to established psychological theory. Examiners expect you to explain why your results make sense in light of what psychologists already know. Every section — rationale, discussion, conclusion — should reference relevant theory.

Key Psychological Theories to Connect

Use these in your Rationale and Discussion
▼

Context-Dependent Memory (Grant et al., 1998)

Memory performance is better when the environment at retrieval matches the environment at encoding — this is the context-dependency effect. Grant et al. (1998) demonstrated this using meaningful prose (a two-page article) read in silent or noisy conditions, with recall and recognition tests given in matching or mismatching conditions. Both test types showed superior performance in matching conditions. This is the central finding your modification builds upon.

→ Connect this in your Rationale (Para 2–3), Discussion (link results to context-dependency), and Conclusion.

Encoding Specificity Principle (Tulving & Thomson, 1973)

Retrieval is most effective when the cues available at retrieval closely match those present during encoding. Environmental context (e.g. background noise or silence) is encoded as part of the memory trace. If the same contextual cues are present at both study and test, retrieval is enhanced — explaining why matching conditions outperform mismatching conditions in Grant et al. (1998).

→ Use this in your Rationale to explain why context-dependency occurs, and in Discussion to interpret your results.

Encoding & Retrieval

Encoding converts information into a memory trace — including contextual features like background noise. Retrieval is accessing stored information. The better the match between encoding and retrieval contexts, the stronger the retrieval cues. In Grant et al. (1998), background noise (or silence) was inadvertently encoded as part of the memory trace, helping or hindering later retrieval depending on whether the test environment matched.

→ Use when explaining why your IV (e.g. study environment) affects the DV (e.g. test score).

Multi-Store Model of Memory (Atkinson & Shiffrin, 1968)

Memory is divided into three stores: sensory memory (brief, automatic), short-term memory (limited capacity ~7±2 items, ~30s duration without rehearsal), and long-term memory (unlimited capacity, long duration). Information moves between stores through attention and rehearsal.

→ Use as background theory in your Rationale (Para 1–2) when defining memory.

Working Memory Model (Baddeley & Hitch, 1974)

Replaces the concept of short-term memory with a more complex system: a central executive (attention controller), phonological loop (verbal/auditory info), visuospatial sketchpad (visual/spatial info), and episodic buffer (integrates information). Relevant if your modification involves verbal vs visual stimuli or different modalities.

→ Use if your modification changes stimulus type or involves a concurrent task.

Types of Variables

Must operationalise IV and DV in your Method and RQ
▼

Independent Variable (IV)

The variable you deliberately manipulate. It has at least two levels/conditions. Must be operationalised — state exactly what each condition involves.

Example: Study environment (silent vs noisy conditions during reading)

Dependent Variable (DV)

The variable you measure — what you expect to change as a result of manipulating the IV. Must be operationalised — state exactly how it is measured and in what units.

Example: Number of items correctly answered on a short-answer or multiple-choice test

Extraneous Variables (EVs)

Any variable other than the IV that could potentially affect the DV. Must be identified and controlled before data collection. If not controlled, they become confounding variables.

Examples: reading time, participant age, prior familiarity with the article topic, headphone volume

Confounding Variables

Extraneous variables that were not controlled and therefore did affect the DV alongside the IV. Identified after data collection. Lower internal validity because you can no longer be confident the IV caused the change in the DV.

Example: participants in one condition happened to be more rested than those in the other

Control Variables

Variables that are deliberately kept constant across conditions to prevent them from becoming confounds. List these in your Method (Design & Variables section).

Examples: same article, same headphones, same instructions read aloud, same time allowed

Levels of measurement matter for statistics: Nominal (categories, e.g. male/female) → Ordinal (ranked order) → Interval (equal gaps, no true zero) → Ratio (equal gaps + true zero, e.g. number of words recalled). The type of data you collect determines which statistical test is appropriate.

Research Designs

State and justify your design in the Method
▼

Repeated Measures (Within-participants)

Each participant experiences all conditions. Advantages: controls participant variables; requires fewer participants. Disadvantages: risk of order effects (practice/fatigue). Control: counterbalancing.

Independent Groups (Between-participants)

Different participants in each condition. Advantages: no order effects. Disadvantages: participant variables may differ between groups; needs more participants. Control: random allocation.

Matched Participants

Participants are paired based on a relevant characteristic (e.g. age, prior memory score) and one from each pair is assigned to each condition. Balances participant variables without order effects.

Types of Limitations

Identify → Describe → Evaluate → Improve
▼
Structure for every limitation: (1) Identify it by name, (2) Describe how it appeared in your study, (3) Evaluate the effect on reliability or validity, (4) Improve — suggest a specific solution.

Sample Bias

↓ External (population) validity

What: Sample doesn't accurately represent the target population (e.g. only students from one year level, one school, one gender). Caused by convenience or volunteer sampling. Improve: Use random or stratified sampling.

Order Effects (repeated measures only)

↓ Internal validity

Practice effect: performance improves simply from doing the task a second time. Fatigue/boredom effect: performance worsens in the second condition due to tiredness. Both are confounding variables. Improve: counterbalancing (half do Condition A then B; half do B then A).

Demand Characteristics

↓ Internal validity

Participants guess the aim of the experiment and alter their behaviour accordingly (either trying to help or sabotage the hypothesis). Improve: single-blind procedure (participants don't know which condition they're in); ethical deception with debriefing.

Lack of Mundane Realism

↓ External validity

The memory task is artificial — memorising a list of words is unlike how memory is used in everyday life. Limits ecological validity. Improve: use more naturalistic stimuli (e.g. meaningful sentences, images, stories).

Participant Variables

↓ Internal validity

Individual differences between participants (age, prior memory ability, fatigue, anxiety) that may influence the DV. Most problematic in independent groups designs. Improve: random allocation to groups; matched participants design.

Situational / Environmental Effects

↓ Internal validity

Uncontrolled environmental factors (noise, lighting, distractions) that vary between conditions or testing sessions and may affect performance. Improve: conduct study in a controlled lab-like setting; standardise the testing environment.

Ceiling / Floor Effect

↓ Internal validity

Ceiling: task too easy — most participants score near the maximum, so differences can't be detected. Floor: task too hard — most score near zero. Both produce skewed distributions and prevent the DV from accurately reflecting the IV's effect. Improve: pilot test the stimulus difficulty; adjust word list length or task complexity.

Useful sentence starters:

"There may be some possible limitations in this study."
"The findings of this study have to be seen in light of some limitations."
"This research is subject to several limitations. First… Second…"

Uncertainty & Types of Error

Discuss in Results and Discussion using data values
▼
Uncertainty = ambiguity that remains in your findings even after analysis. It doesn't mean your results are wrong — it means you acknowledge the limits of how confident you can be. Always reference specific SD values, confidence intervals, or error bar overlap.

Random Error

Unpredictable, unsystematic fluctuations that affect results differently each time. Affects precision and internal reliability. Caused by: instrument limitations, environmental variations, inconsistent procedure. Reduce by: larger sample size, repeated measurements, standardised procedure.

Systematic Error

Consistent deviation from the true value in the same direction every time. Affects accuracy and internal validity. Caused by: flawed instruments, experimenter bias, environmental interference. Reduce by: knowing instrument limitations; calibrating tools; double-blind procedure.

Personal Error

Mistakes or miscalculations made by the researcher (e.g. misreading a score, timing error). Should NOT be included in your data — if identified, redo that part of the experiment. Do not report personal errors as limitations.

Writing about uncertainty — use this template:

"The [condition] scores had a greater dispersion (SD = __) than the [condition] scores (SD = __), which increases the uncertainty of the results for the [condition] group and reduces confidence in conclusions drawn about that condition."

Writing about error bars and confidence intervals:

  • If error bars do not overlap → suggests a statistically significant difference between conditions
  • If error bars overlap considerably → suggests the difference between conditions may not be significant
  • Wider error bars = greater variability in that condition = more uncertainty
  • Always link your comment about error bars back to your p-value

Reliability & Validity — Four Types Each

Discuss all four types with strengths AND weaknesses
▼

Reliability — the extent to which results are consistent across repeated measurements

External Reliability — Replicability

Can the same results be obtained by repeating the experiment under identical conditions? High replicability = high reliability. Improve: standardised procedure, clear operationalisation of variables.

External Reliability — Test-Retest

Repeat the same test on the same participants after a period of time — a reliable instrument produces similar scores. Psychology tests are susceptible to mood, fatigue, and context changes. Improve: use precise, standardised instruments.

External Reliability — Inter-Rater Reliability

Consistency of ratings across different observers. Relevant if your study involves subjective scoring or observation. Improve: train raters; use a detailed scoring rubric; clearly operationalise variables.

Internal Reliability — Internal Consistency

All items or measures in an instrument consistently assess the same construct. Random errors reduce internal consistency. Improve: use well-validated instruments; ensure word list items are equivalent in difficulty.

Validity — the extent to which the study measures what it claims to measure

Internal Validity

The extent to which changes in the DV were caused by the IV and not by confounding variables. High internal validity means you can be confident the IV caused the effect. Threats: confounding variables, order effects, demand characteristics, investigator effects.

External Validity — Population Validity

Can findings generalise to people outside the sample? A convenience sample from one school limits generalisation to broader populations. Improve: random or stratified sampling; larger sample.

External Validity — Ecological Validity

Can findings generalise to real-world settings? Lab-based memory tasks may not reflect how memory works in everyday life. Improve: use naturalistic stimuli; conduct study in participants' normal environment.

Mundane Realism

The degree to which the experimental task resembles real-life activities. Low mundane realism limits ecological validity. Memorising a word list under laboratory conditions has low mundane realism. Improve: use tasks that reflect everyday memory demands.

🔗 Where to connect theory in your report

② Rationale

Define context-dependent memory and encoding specificity. Explain how Grant et al. (1998) demonstrated the context-dependency effect and why your modification extends or refines it.

④ Method

Link your choice of IV and DV to theory — explain why manipulating this variable should affect memory, based on context-dependent memory and encoding specificity.

⑥ Discussion

Explain whether your results support or contradict the context-dependency effect. Link directly to Grant et al. (1998) — did your matching conditions outperform your mismatching conditions? If not, discuss why.

⑦ Conclusion

Explicitly state whether your findings support or challenge the theoretical framework. Don't just answer the RQ — situate it within the broader psychology of memory.
